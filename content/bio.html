BIO (I swear I tried to keep it short)

In the early nineties my family finally got its first computer, an AppleII+ (which even then was pretty outdated if I remember correctly), but I fell in love with it and taught myself to program some simple things in BASIC. When we eventually got a decent (for the time) PC running Windows 3.1, I taught myself QBasic. Throughout high school I was certain I would go on to major in computer science. That didn't happen.

I dropped out of college two seperate times and spent the next 10-15 years working on being a writer of short stories (of which I have published a few) and an experimental electronic music composer/producer (which got me some unpaid soundtrack work and a listing on IMDB). But I paid the bills by working third shift at a gas station for a long time, then got married and had a baby and became manager/bookkeeper at a small group of privately owned gas station/convenience stores. That was not how I wanted to spend 40+ hours of my week.

While there were times in those intervening years that I felt the pull of code and went back to mess with various languages such as C/C++ and BlitzBasic, it was not until Februrary of 2014, after reexamining my life and hearing a news piece on NPR about Codecademy that I decided to dive back into the field I perhaps should have never abandoned. I did not have the time or money to go back to a "real" school, so I took a number of Saylor.org courses, earning certificates in basic computer science, discrete math, and data structures/algorithms (see above). But it still wasn't what I wanted exactly. Sure I was picking up some Java and C++, but I wanted to build things, higher-level things I could share with other people, not just learn theory and implement a linked list. (Not to say that stuff isn't important, I'm happy to have gotten that background).

Finally, I discovered a few websites (such as this guide and The Odin Project) that laid out a curriculum for learning web development. I mostly followed Odin at first, learning Ruby with the intention of also eventually learning Rails, and that was great fun. Ruby brought back my love of coding tenfold. But I had to think practically, too, especially after being laid off, and in October 2014 I decided to focus on front-end development, specifically doing my best to learn the quirky and sometimes infuriating Javascript (also using this excellent book) in the hope of being able to land an entry/junior level FE web development job. (In the future I do intend to go back and learn server-side programming as well, and I'm more likely to look into Node than Ruby/Rails at this point, but only time will tell for sure.)

So that's where I am as of this writing. All of the projects in the portfolio portion of this site have been made in the last few months. They aren't perfect, but I believe they show that I have learned quite a lot...and I am hungry to learn so much more, because despite the hours and hours of work I have put into my education, it has also been great fun.

About this site

This website was hand-coded in HTML, CSS, and Javascript, using no templates or frameworks or libraries other than jQuery. I am not a graphic designer, but I did my best (after multiple redesigns that left my CSS looking like a tangled mess) to make a clean site with a minimal but unique look. There is still much work to be done to ensure its cross-browser compatibility and the fluidity of the display on screens of varying sizes, but if you would like to see the source it can be found on my github.
